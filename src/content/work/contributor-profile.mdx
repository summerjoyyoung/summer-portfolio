This was a contributor profile page for an internal platform where engineers publish, version, and adopt shared front-end patterns. The brief looked small — the page didn't exist, and the job was to make it. The real work was deciding what the page was _for_.

**My role:** Sole designer — problem framing, audit, evaluation, and high-fidelity design.
**Tools:** Figma.

## The reframe

Before designing, I built a quick working baseline of the page to map what user data was actually available — which fields existed, which were unreliable, and which I'd have to create. Left to defaults, that baseline looked like a social profile: avatar, contact details, favorites, view counts. It treated the contributor like a _visitor to a website_. But on an internal contribution platform, one question matters above all others: **can I trust this person's work enough to build on it?** A profile here isn't a vanity object — it's a _trust signal_. That single reframe drove every decision: cut consumption metrics, demote low-value identity, and promote the signals that establish credibility and maintenance.

## The method

I pressure-tested the thesis through three independent lenses rather than one:

- **Content audit** — annotated every element of the baseline with a cut / revise / promote / keep rationale, forcing each pixel to defend itself against the trust thesis.
- **Heuristic evaluation** — surfaced the states the baseline ignored: self-view vs. peer-view, empty states, and edit-mode boundaries.
- **Competitive analysis** — studied how leading platforms handle contributor identity, then deliberately _rejected_ public-social-graph conventions in favor of internal-tool norms.

## Designing under a moving target

Partway through, the intended audience expanded dramatically — which strained the premise the entire design rested on. Rather than scramble a response to a target that hadn't settled, I made a scoping call: ship the version validated for the core user, and treat the audience expansion as the next research question. The redesign shipped in the product's launch, largely intact — turning the design from an untested concept into a falsifiable bet that real usage could now answer.

## What I'd talk through

The honest version of this project isn't a clean redesign — it's a clear thesis placed under uncertainty, with a deliberate plan to find out if it was right. The full case study covers the reframe in depth, the three-method process with artifacts, the moment the mandate shifted, the decision to ship anyway, and the specific v2 hypothesis early access is positioned to test.
